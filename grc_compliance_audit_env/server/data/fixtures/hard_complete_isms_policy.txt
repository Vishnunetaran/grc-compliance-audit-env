INFORMATION SECURITY MANAGEMENT SYSTEM (ISMS) POLICY v3.1
Organisation: Vertex Cloud Solutions Pte Ltd
Classification: Internal Use Only
Effective Date: 01 April 2025
Review Cycle: Annual
Frameworks: ISO 27001:2022 | NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 | SOC 2 TSC

This document constitutes the overarching Information Security Management System
(ISMS) policy for Vertex Cloud Solutions Pte Ltd ("the Organisation"). It
encompasses ten domains required for certification and compliance readiness across
ISO 27001:2022, NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5, and SOC 2 Trust Services Criteria.

================================================================================

SECTION 1: SECURITY GOVERNANCE AND MANAGEMENT DIRECTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.1 Policy Framework

The Organisation is committed to maintaining a comprehensive, documented
information security policy framework aligned with internationally recognised
standards. The Board of Directors and Executive Leadership Team provide active
oversight and direction for information security, ensuring adequate resource
allocation and strategic alignment with business objectives.

1.2 Roles and Responsibilities

The Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) holds primary accountability for
the ISMS and reports directly to the Chief Executive Officer. The CISO is
supported by the Security Steering Committee, which consists of representatives
from IT, Legal, Compliance, HR, and Operations. Information security roles and
responsibilities are formally documented and communicated to all personnel
as part of the onboarding process and annual policy acceptance cycle.

1.3 Governance Structure

The Security Steering Committee meets quarterly to review security posture,
approve policy changes, and oversee risk treatment decisions. Management
demonstrates commitment to information security through resource allocation,
policy endorsement, and participating in annual security reviews. Disciplinary
procedures exist for personnel who fail to comply with security policies.

1.4 Awareness and Compliance

All employees and contractors must acknowledge and accept all applicable security
policies annually. Compliance with this policy and relevant sub-policies is
mandatory. Non-compliance may result in disciplinary action, up to and including
termination of employment or legal proceedings where applicable.

SECTION 2: ASSET MANAGEMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.1 Asset Inventory

The Organisation maintains a comprehensive inventory of all information assets,
including hardware (servers, workstations, network devices), software (licensed
applications, custom-developed systems), data assets (databases, file stores),
and cloud services. The inventory is stored in the Configuration Management
Database (CMDB) and reviewed quarterly by the IT Asset Management team.

2.2 Asset Classification

All information assets are classified according to the Organisation's four-tier
classification scheme: Confidential, Internal Use Only, Public, and Restricted.
Asset owners are responsible for assigning the appropriate classification at the
time of asset registration. Classification must be reviewed when the asset's
purpose, content, or regulatory context changes.

2.3 Acceptable Use

All personnel must use information assets only for authorised business purposes.
Personal use of Organisation assets is permitted only within the limits defined
in the Acceptable Use Policy (AUP). Prohibited activities include but are not
limited to: bypassing security controls, accessing unauthorised systems,
transferring Organisation data to personal cloud storage, and installing
unapproved software.

2.4 Cloud Asset Management

Cloud service usage must be approved by the CISO and registered in the asset
inventory. Information security requirements for cloud services are defined
in the Cloud Security Policy, covering data residency, encryption, access
controls, and vendor risk assessment. Shadow IT (use of unapproved cloud
services) is strictly prohibited and monitored via the Data Loss Prevention
(DLP) platform.

SECTION 3: ACCESS CONTROL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.1 Access Principles

Access to all Organisation systems and information assets is governed by the
principles of least privilege and need-to-know. Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)
is implemented across all critical systems. Access rights are provisioned
through a formal request and approval workflow, countersigned by the asset owner
and the requester's line manager.

3.2 Identity and Access Management

User identities are managed through the Organisation's centralised Identity
and Access Management (IAM) platform. User accounts are created, modified, and
deprovisioned through automated workflows triggered by HR events. All accounts
are subject to semi-annual access reviews conducted by system owners.

3.3 Privileged Access Management

Privileged accounts — including domain administrator, local administrator,
database administrator, and cloud console root credentials — are managed through
a dedicated Privileged Access Management (PAM) solution. Just-in-time (JIT)
access elevation is required for all privileged operations. All privileged
sessions are fully recorded and retained for twelve months.

3.4 Remote Access

Remote access is permitted only via the Organisation-approved Virtual Private
Network (VPN). Remote sessions are restricted to Organisation-managed endpoints
only, which must comply with the endpoint security baseline (antivirus, disk
encryption, screen lock).

NOTE TO AUDITOR (GAP 1): This section does not currently specify a requirement
for multi-factor authentication (MFA) for any access tier, including remote
access and privileged access. This is a CRITICAL gap under ISO 27001:2022
control A.8.5 (Secure Authentication), NIST control IA-2 (Identification and
Authentication), and SOC 2 criterion CC6.6 (Authentication). An MFA requirement
must be added before the Organisation can achieve certification.

SECTION 4: CRYPTOGRAPHY AND DATA PROTECTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.1 Cryptographic Policy

The Organisation employs cryptographic controls to protect the confidentiality,
integrity, and authenticity of information in transit and at rest. The use of
cryptography is governed by the Cryptographic Standards Policy, which defines
approved algorithms, key lengths, and key management procedures.

4.2 Encryption in Transit

All data classified as Confidential or Restricted must be encrypted during
transmission using TLS 1.2 or higher. Use of obsolete protocols (SSLv3, TLS 1.0,
TLS 1.1) is prohibited and blocked at the network boundary. Email communications
containing Confidential information must use S/MIME or PGP encryption.

4.3 Encryption at Rest

Databases containing Confidential or Confidential data are encrypted using
AES-256 at the storage layer. Laptop hard drives are encrypted using BitLocker
(Windows) or FileVault (macOS) with organisational key recovery escrow. Backup
media is encrypted before offsite transfer.

4.4 Key Management

Cryptographic keys are managed by the IT Security team using an approved Key
Management System (KMS). Key rotation schedules are defined per algorithm and
use case. Private keys are stored in Hardware Security Modules (HSMs) where
feasible. Key usage is logged and audited quarterly.

SECTION 5: PHYSICAL AND ENVIRONMENTAL SECURITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.1 Physical Security Perimeters

The Organisation's primary data centre and office facilities are protected by
defined physical security perimeters. Entry to secure areas is controlled via
proximity badge access. All entry attempts are logged by the Physical Access
Control System (PACS). Visitors must sign in at reception, be escorted at all
times within secure areas, and sign out on departure.

5.2 Physical Monitoring

CCTV surveillance covers all entry points, server rooms, and critical utility
areas. Footage is retained for a minimum of 90 days. Motion detection alerts
are configured to trigger automated notifications to the Security Operations
Centre outside of business hours.

5.3 Environmental Controls

Server rooms and data centre spaces are equipped with precision air conditioning
units, uninterruptible power supplies (UPS), and diesel backup generators capable
of sustaining operations for 72 hours. Fire suppression systems use clean-agent
gas (FM-200) to protect equipment. Temperature and humidity are monitored
continuously with automated alerting.

5.4 Equipment Security

All Organisation-owned equipment used off-premises (laptops, mobile devices,
USB drives) must comply with the Mobile Device Policy. Disposal of equipment
containing data storage media must follow the Media Sanitisation Procedure,
which mandates cryptographic erasure or physical destruction.

SECTION 6: OPERATIONS SECURITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.1 Malware Protection

All endpoints and servers are protected by Organisation-approved anti-malware
software. Anti-malware definitions are updated automatically with a maximum
permissible latency of four hours. Malware detections trigger automatic quarantine
and immediate SOC notification. Email and web gateway scanning is applied to
all inbound and outbound traffic.

6.2 Logging and Monitoring

All critical systems, network devices, security appliances, and cloud platforms
generate audit logs. Logs are centralised in the Security Information and Event
Management (SIEM) platform and retained for a minimum of twelve months. The SOC
performs continuous monitoring of the SIEM with automated alerting for high-
severity events. Log integrity is protected against tampering.

6.3 Change Management

All changes to production systems, applications, network configurations, and
security controls must follow the Organisation's Change Management Process (CMP).
Changes are categorised as Standard, Normal, or Emergency. Normal changes require
review and approval by the Change Advisory Board (CAB) prior to implementation.
Emergency changes must be retrospectively approved within 24 hours. Change
records are maintained for audit purposes.

NOTE TO AUDITOR (GAP 2): This section does not define a vulnerability scanning
or patch management programme. There is no defined scanning frequency, no patch
SLA by criticality, and no reference to a penetration testing schedule. This is a
HIGH gap under ISO 27001:2022 control A.8.8 (Management of Technical
Vulnerabilities), NIST control RA-5 (Vulnerability Monitoring and Scanning),
and SOC 2 criterion CC7.1 (Detection and Monitoring of New Vulnerabilities).

SECTION 7: INCIDENT MANAGEMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7.1 Incident Response Programme

The Organisation maintains a formal Incident Response Plan (IRP) consistent with
industry best practices. The CISO serves as the Incident Response Owner. The
Security Operations Centre (SOC) is the first point of contact for all reported
security events. The IRP is tested via annual tabletop exercises and updated
following any major incident.

7.2 Incident Reporting

All employees must report suspected or confirmed security incidents immediately
via the SOC hotline or the incident reporting portal. Incidents must not be
disclosed externally without CISO or Legal approval. Regulatory reporting
obligations (e.g., MAS TRM, PDPA breach notification) are managed by the CISO
in coordination with Legal and Compliance.

7.3 Incident Handling

Upon receipt of an incident report, the SOC performs initial triage and assigns
a severity level. Confirmed incidents trigger the incident response workflow:
containment, evidence preservation, eradication, recovery, and post-incident
review. The SOC maintains an incident log for all events, regardless of severity.

NOTE TO AUDITOR (GAP 3): This section does not define incident classification
criteria for determining what constitutes a "security incident" versus a routine
"security event." Without formal classification criteria, the Organisation cannot
consistently trigger appropriate response procedures. This is a HIGH gap under
ISO 27001:2022 control A.5.24 (Incident Management Planning and Preparation),
NIST control IR-4 (Incident Handling), and SOC 2 criterion CC7.3 (Evaluation of
Security Events). Formal incident classification criteria must be documented.

SECTION 8: BUSINESS CONTINUITY AND RESILIENCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8.1 Business Continuity Planning

The Organisation maintains a Business Continuity Plan (BCP) and a Disaster
Recovery Plan (DRP) to ensure the continued operation of critical business
processes in the event of disruption. The BCP is reviewed and tested annually
via simulation exercises. Business Impact Analysis (BIA) is conducted to identify
critical systems, maximum tolerable downtime (MTD), and recovery priorities.

8.2 ICT Continuity

Critical IT systems are designated with Recovery Time Objectives (RTO) and
Recovery Point Objectives (RPO) defined in the Disaster Recovery Plan. The
Organisation operates a geographically separated secondary data centre for
disaster recovery purposes. Failover capabilities are tested semi-annually.

8.3 Data Backup

Data backup is performed on all critical systems. Backups are stored in
geographically separate locations. Backup integrity is verified monthly via
restore tests. Backup media is encrypted before offsite transfer.

NOTE TO AUDITOR (GAP 4): This section does not specify the backup frequency or
schedule for any system. Without a defined backup frequency — such as daily
incremental and weekly full backups — the Organisation cannot demonstrate that
its RPO commitments are achievable. This is a HIGH gap under ISO 27001:2022
control A.5.30 (ICT Readiness for Business Continuity), NIST control CP-9
(System Backup), and SOC 2 criterion A-1 (Availability). Backup frequency must
be explicitly defined per system criticality tier.

SECTION 9: SUPPLIER AND VENDOR MANAGEMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
9.1 Supplier Risk Framework

The Organisation recognises the risk posed by third-party suppliers, vendors,
and service providers who have access to Organisation systems, data, or
facilities. All suppliers are subject to an initial security risk assessment
prior to engagement and periodic re-assessment thereafter.

9.2 Contractual Requirements

All supplier agreements must include information security clauses covering:
data handling and classification obligations, incident notification requirements
(within 24 hours of discovery), right-to-audit provisions, compliance with
applicable laws and regulations, and security baseline requirements for
personnel with access to Organisation data.

9.3 Ongoing Monitoring

Tier-1 suppliers (those with access to Confidential or Restricted data) are
subject to annual security reassessment. The Procurement team is responsible
for maintaining the Approved Supplier Register and raising security deviations
to the CISO.

NOTE TO AUDITOR (GAP 5): This section does not describe the process for
conducting third-party security assessments — it only states that assessments
occur. There is no defined methodology, questionnaire, or scoring criteria for
evaluating supplier security posture. This is a MEDIUM gap under ISO 27001:2022
control A.5.19 (Information Security in Supplier Relationships), NIST control
SR-3 (Supply Chain Controls and Processes), and SOC 2 criterion CC9.2 (Vendor
and Business Partner Risk Management). A formal supplier assessment procedure
must be documented.

SECTION 10: COMPLIANCE, AUDIT, AND LEGAL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
10.1 Regulatory Compliance

The Organisation operates under multiple regulatory and legal obligations,
including the Singapore Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA), MAS Technology
Risk Management (TRM) Guidelines, and SOC 2 Type II audit requirements.
The Compliance team, in coordination with Legal, maintains an up-to-date register
of applicable regulations and monitors for changes that may affect the ISMS.

10.2 Internal Audit Programme

An annual internal audit of the ISMS is conducted by the Internal Audit function
or an approved external party. The audit programme covers all ISMS domains and
verifies compliance with this policy, applicable controls, and regulatory
requirements. Findings are reported to the Security Steering Committee and
tracked through to closure.

10.3 Intellectual Property and Licensing

All software used by the Organisation must be properly licensed. Use of
unlicensed software is prohibited. The IT Asset Management team maintains a
software licence inventory and performs periodic reconciliation to ensure
compliance with licence terms.

10.4 Privacy and Personal Data Protection

The Organisation collects, processes, and stores personal data in accordance
with the PDPA and applicable privacy regulations. A Data Protection Officer (DPO)
is appointed to oversee data protection activities. Privacy impact assessments
are conducted for new systems or processes that involve personal data collection.

10.5 Evidence and Records

Audit evidence, compliance records, and policy acknowledgement logs are retained
per the Data Retention Schedule. Records must be protected against loss,
destruction, falsification, and unauthorised access. Access to compliance records
is restricted to authorised personnel only.
